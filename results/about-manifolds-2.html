Does the shape scale?

Toto-2 is sold on one promise the previous generation couldn't keep: it scales — train it bigger and it forecasts better, the clean way large language models do. So here is the obvious question for an interpretability tool. As the model grows from 4 million to 2.5 billion parameters, do its manifolds get cleaner? We ran the identical factor sweeps from Part 1 on the whole size ladder. The answer isn't one curve — it's four very different ones.

← Part 1 · The shape of a signal Open the full dashboard (45 models) →

In Part 1 we treated a frozen model's representation as geometry: sweep one generative factor across a fine grid, average the activations at each step into a centroid, and the centroids trace a shape. The question was never just whether a factor is decodable — it was whether the representation is organised the way the process that generated the data is. One model, Toto-2.0-2.5B, carried the whole tour.

This time we vary one thing — size — and hold everything else fixed. Toto-2.0 ships as a clean ladder of five checkpoints, from a 4-million-parameter sketch to the 2.5-billion-parameter flagship, and Datadog's headline claim is that quality climbs smoothly up that ladder. If that's true of the forecasts, is it true of the shapes?

One thing never changes across the rungs: the front-end. Every size reads the signal in the same 32-sample patches and standardises every window the same way. Hold onto that — it decides almost everything that follows. The answer to "does the geometry scale" turns out to be: for one factor it's already perfect at 4M; for one it is destroyed before the first layer, and scale can't bring it back; for one it genuinely sharpens — then stops; and for one the smallest model ties the largest. Four cases, four fates.

    The setupFive rungs, one tokenizer

    Here is the ladder. Width and depth grow together — from 256 channels and 5 layers to 2048 channels and 49 — for a roughly 600× span in parameters. The patch size is 32 for all of them.

    One housekeeping note that matters for everything below. Part 1 showed that a factor's cleanest geometry usually lives in the middle of the network, not at the output. So for each rung we score every factor at its best layer, not its last. Taking the final layer would handicap all five models the same way and hide the real differences — and, as Case 4 shows, it would throw away the most interesting part of the story.

    Case 1 · sine_amplitudeThe door scaling can't reopen

    Amplitude should be the easiest factor in the suite. Make a sine louder, and louder, and louder; the representation ought to slide along a tidy line. On the Toto-2 ladder it does no such thing. Amplitude isn't hard here — it's simply gone, and it's gone the same way at every size. Below, the same sweep machinery that drew a clean phase ring in Part 1: phase (top) becomes a ring at every size; amplitude (bottom) becomes a point.

    Why? Toto standardises every input window by its own scale before it patches it. A pure sine's standard deviation is proportional to its amplitude, so dividing it out cancels amplitude exactly: a quiet sine and a loud one become the same normalised wave. The transformer is handed an input with the amplitude already removed — before layer 0 ever runs.

    So how blind is blind? We measured the spread of the 1,024 amplitude centroids in the full 64-dimensional space. It is 0.000 — bit-for-bit identical — at layer 0 and at every layer after it, for all five sizes. 5-NN recall sits at 0.006, exactly chance, and never moves. There is no manifold; there is a point. Crucially, this is not a depth problem the small models simply "haven't grown into" — the input is identical, so every one of the 2.5B model's 49 layers is identically empty.

    You might conclude amplitude is just un-representable from a standardised window. It isn't. Plenty of models lay it out cleanly — and so did Toto's own previous generation.

    The lesson is the cleanest in the whole post. Scaling can reopen many doors. It cannot reopen one the tokenizer has already shut.

    Case 2 · spike_time_fracWhere scaling actually pays off — and where it stops

    Now a factor the tokenizer keeps but folds: the position of a single-sample spike. In Part 1 its layer-0 manifold was a tangled triangle — the 32-sample patch grid showing through — that the deeper layers patiently disentangle into a ring. That's exactly the kind of work extra capacity might help with. Here it is, as 5-NN recall (how sharply the model resolves neighbouring positions) against depth, one curve per size.

    You can watch the manifold itself sharpen. Each panel is one size's spike manifold at its best layer — the folded triangle of the small models resolving into the clean, ordered loop of the large ones.

    And then it stops. 1B and 2.5B don't beat 313M — the last 8× of parameters add no new shape. It isn't that the small models are merely too shallow to reach the peak: compared at the same relative depth (30% of the way down), 4M scores 0.39 while 313M scores 0.70. The small model sits below the others everywhere, not just at the end — this is a ceiling set by capacity, and 313M already reaches it.

    Scaling works here — until 313M. After that the curve is flat.

    Case 3 · sine_frequency_hzWhen the smallest model ties the largest

    Frequency is the factor Toto has to build. Phase arrives as a clean circle at layer 0; frequency starts as a tangle and gets straightened out across depth (Part 1). More depth, surely, should mean a straighter result — and the 2.5B model has ten times the layers of the 4M one. Here is global geometry (geodesic isometry) against depth, per size.

    Across the ladder the best global score traces a gentle inverted-U — it peaks at 313M (0.989) and the giants slip back. And locally it's worse than a tie: 5-NN recall among neighbouring frequencies falls monotonically with size, 0.842 → 0.808, and at matched depth the 4M model sits above the 2.5B one.

    More layers, the same shape — and slightly blurrier neighbours.

    Case 4 · depthThe clean layer is in the middle

    Three cases kept raising the same question: where in the network does the clean geometry live? For every structured factor the answer is the same — and it is not the output. Here is the 2.5B model alone, with four factors' local resolution plotted against depth.

    Which puts the whole scaling story in one sentence. A bigger Toto-2 doesn't carry a factor's geometry further along — it rebuilds the same sweet spot at the same relative depth, and then has more layers in which to walk away from it. You'd instinctively read out the last layer; for these factors the middle is where the model understood the signal best.

    Capacity raises the ceiling a factor can reach. It does not decide which factors are in the room — the tokenizer already did that.

    What this shows — and what it doesn't

    • It's representation geometry across a single family's size ladder, not a forecasting-accuracy benchmark. A model can scale beautifully on loss while a given factor's shape saturates, stalls, or was never present.
    • "Best layer" means the best of all layers for that factor. We use it precisely because the clean layer is usually in the middle (Case 4); reporting the last layer would understate every model.
    • The amplitude and slope results are about the tokenizer, not the architecture's ceiling. The same transformer on a tokenizer that kept scale (Part 1's LeNEPA contrast) would tell a different story.
    • Everything here is one model family. The methods generalise, but these specific verdicts are Toto-2's; the dashboard lets you run the same comparison on others.

    Open research · a community effort

    This is research in progress — come build it with us

    Everything here is early, evolving, and deliberately open. Representation geometry for time-series models is far too interesting — and too big — to keep to ourselves. Collaboration, questions, pushback, and wild ideas are all very welcome.

    • Want your model's whole size ladder in the benchmark? We're happy to add more architectures, checkpoints, and families.
    • Have a scaling story we missed? Suggest factors, sweeps, and comparisons.
    • Spotted a bug or a misleading metric? Tell us — we'll fix it.
    • Want to dig in together? We're keen to co-investigate and publish as a group.
