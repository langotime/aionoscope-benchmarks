Manifolds in motion

The first post froze a model and walked around it — across layers, across factors — to read the shape of what it had already learned. This one adds the axis we left out: time. We ran the same manifold suite on every checkpoint of one model, from the first thousand training steps to the last. The natural guess is that the geometry just sharpens with training. It doesn't. A network spends its length building the shape of one factor and quietly tearing down another — and the usual instrument, a linear probe, is blind to the difference.

In the first post we treated a trained model as a finished object: sweep a generative factor across a fine grid, freeze the activations, average them into centroids, and score the shape that path traces through representation space. A phase that closes into a ring, a spike whose position stretches into a line, a slope that fans out along an axis — geometry as a read-out of what the model understands.

But a checkpoint is a freeze-frame of something that was moving. So we kept the protocol identical and changed exactly one thing — we ran it on every checkpoint of a single model, LeNEPA-CauKer2M, all 220 of them, from training step 1,000 to 220,000. Seven factors, nine layers, 220 snapshots: a frame-by-frame movie of a representation assembling itself.

The intuition is that more training simply sharpens the picture. It does not. Training spends its length doing two opposite things at once — building the geometry of one factor while dismantling another's — and a linear probe, the usual instrument, can't tell the two apart.

Three cases, each picking up exactly where an example from Part 1 left off. The charts are live: they stream one small bundle of precomputed artifacts and render as you scroll.

Case 1 · spike_time_frac A network unfolds its own tokenizer

Example 2 of the first post followed a single spike sliding across the window and watched Toto turn a folded patch-grid into a clean ring as the signal climbed through its layers. LeNEPA performs the same untangling — but you can watch it happen over training, at one fixed layer.

Hold the depth constant at layer 7, a deep layer, and rewind to step 1,000. The spike manifold there is a genuine multi-dimensional tangle: its variance is spread across five-plus directions, only 41% of it lands in the flat 2-D view, and the two ends of the sweep fold back onto each other. The order is in there — you can thread the path in the right sequence (geodesic order ≈ 0.96) — but it is nothing like a straight ruler (straight-line order ≈ 0.41). Now press play on training.

The numbers behind those frames are blunt. The effective dimensionality of the layer-7 manifold — how many independent directions it really needs — collapses from about 7 to 1; the share of variance in one axis climbs from 25% to 99%; straight-line order rises from 0.41 to 1.00 and the stress that measures how badly distances are distorted falls from 0.52 to 0.02. Most of it is done inside the first ten thousand steps.

That layer-0 control is the whole argument for learned. The convolutional tokenizer folds the spike onto its patch grid the instant the signal arrives, and no amount of training changes that — its manifold is byte-for-byte as tangled at the end as at the beginning. Everything that straightens out is built by the transformer blocks above it, and they build it on a schedule you can watch.

One honest caveat keeps it from being a fairy tale: even at step 220k the immediate neighbours stay a little scrambled (5-NN recall never gets high), because a fine within-patch ripple — the same coarse-vs-fine split from Example 2 — rides along the clean line. The model promotes the coarse spike position into a perfect ruler and leaves the sub-patch wobble as texture on top. A ruler with a ripple, but a ruler.

Case 2 · linear_trend_slope A network learns to forget

Example 3 ended on a reassuring note. Toto's front-end standardizes a window and divides the trend's slope clean away; LeNEPA's tokenizer, which normalizes only locally, keeps the scale that Toto throws out. Watch the very same factor across training, though, and the reassurance curdles: the tokenizer keeps the scale, and then the transformer spends the next two hundred thousand steps learning to throw it away again.

At layer 5, the slope manifold's clean geometry doesn't arrive and stay — it blooms and then erodes. By about 20–55k steps the centroids have spread into a long, well-ordered arc: the two extreme-slope ends sit ~620 typical steps apart and the order along the sweep is near-perfect (monotone order 0.97). Train on, and the arc folds back up: by 220k that spread has more than halved — a 2.2× squeeze — and the order has slumped to 0.63.

Here is the same manifold drawn at its best and its worst — the clean, stretched arc near its peak, and the squeezed, scrambled version after another 185k steps. Both are coloured by the slope they encode; both hand a linear probe a perfect R².

Is the model just getting worse? No — and that's the tell. Over the very same 170k steps, amplitude and phase stay clean, and the spike from Case 1 is busy improving. Only the scale-like factor erodes. What you are watching is the representation learning an invariance: for a forecaster that standardizes its inputs, the absolute slope of a ramp is a nuisance, and the network slowly squeezes it out of the geometry — keeping just enough to stay decodable while spending its capacity on shapes that earn their keep.

This is Part 1's central lesson — decodable is not the same as organized — turned into a movie. A probe reports a flat, perfect line across all 220 checkpoints and would have you believe nothing happened. The geometry tells you the model built a clean slope ruler, thought better of it, and took it back apart.

Case 3 · all seven factors Seven factors, one training run

Put every factor on one chart — best-layer straight-line order against training step — and the two cases above snap into a single picture with three clean regimes.

Born clean — organized from step 1k Built — rises from the floor Built, then discarded — rises, then falls

The shape of the chart is the headline. Almost everything a model needs is present within the first one or two thousand steps; the long tail of training is not spent sharpening those lines but doing the two harder, opposite jobs — constructing the geometry of a folded, stubborn factor and dissolving the geometry of a nuisance one. The flat top lines and the rising blue line and the falling red line are all the same optimizer, on the same data, at the same time.

Takeaway A checkpoint is a freeze-frame of a negotiation

Two practical things fall out of the movie. First, which checkpoint you probe matters: the slope manifold you'd analyze at 50k steps and the one at 220k are geometrically different objects, even though both score a perfect decodability R². If you study a single checkpoint, you are reading one frame of an ongoing negotiation between the factors competing for the model's representational space.

Second, the instrument decides what you can see. A linear probe — the default tool for "does the model represent X?" — reported every factor here as fine, at every checkpoint. It never flagged the spike being painstakingly assembled, or the slope being deliberately taken apart. Geometry does. If you want to know not just whether a factor is present but whether the model is currently building it up or tearing it down, you have to look at the shape.
