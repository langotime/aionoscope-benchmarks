Where linear probes go blind

A colleague read Part 1 and asked the right question: how does this geometry compare to the tool everyone already trusts — the linear probe — and what does it catch that the probe doesn't? Fair. So here are the two diagnostics, side by side, on the same factors and the same models. The punchline: a probe score is a single number, and a low number has two opposite causes the probe can't tell apart. The geometry can.

← Part 1 · The shape of a signal Open the full dashboard (45 models) →

In Part 1 we read a frozen model's representation as geometry: sweep one generative factor across a fine grid, average the activations at each step into a centroid, and watch the centroids trace a shape. The recurring theme was that being decodable is not the same as being organised — a factor can be perfectly present and still invisible to a straight line.

That theme deserves a fair fight. The main Aionoscope benchmark already runs the standard instrument: a pooled linear probe — a single linear readout trained to recover a factor from the frozen features, scored by R² (regression) or AUC (classification), at each layer. It is fast, low-assumption, and excellent at one job: telling you whether a factor is linearly accessible. The trouble is what you conclude when it says no.

A probe asks one narrow question: is there a single straight line along which this factor reads off? When the answer is a low R², it could mean the factor isn't there — or that it is there, bent into a shape no line can read. Those are opposite facts about the model, and the probe reports them with the same number. We'll walk three cases that cover every way the two diagnostics can disagree — probe low and wrong, probe low and right, probe high and incomplete — and in each one the geometry says what the probe cannot.

Contents

    The setupWhat a probe can — and can't — say

    Run the probe across 37 model-plus-adapter systems and a clear pattern appears. Shape and timing factors come back almost perfectly: frequency, event position, duty cycle, and edge sharpness all land at R² ≈ 0.99. Magnitude factors — absolute amplitude, DC offset, noise level — sit at zero, because most tokenizers standardise each window and divide scale away before the transformer sees it. Read the floor of that chart and you'd write off amplitude and offset. You'd be right: the model really did discard them.

    And then there is phase. It is the single lowest-scoring factor that is not a magnitude — median R² 0.10, sitting in the basement with the things the model threw away. Hold that thought: Case 1 shows the model represents phase perfectly. The probe just can't read it.

    Case 1 · probe low, and wrongPhase: the circle a straight line can't read

    Phase runs 0 → 2π and then it is back where it started: phase 0 and phase 2π are the same signal. A model that understands phase should lay it out as a circle. And every model does — sweep the phase of a sine and the centroids trace a closed loop, returning to their start, with the colours running cleanly all the way around (circular-order ≈ 0.99 across families).

    Yet the probe reads ≈ 0 for all of them. The reason is mechanical, not mysterious. The probe is a bare linear layer trained with mean-squared error, and it is handed phase as a plain number on 0 … 2π — so the loss treats 0 and 2π as maximally far apart, and the only fit that minimises it collapses to the mean, ≈ π. A straight line can't go around a circle. Tellingly, the geometry pipeline knows this: for a circular factor it refuses to report an R² at all (it is undefined for a wrapping variable) and measures order and closure instead. The probe has no such guardrail — it returns 0.10 and you draw the wrong conclusion.

    Case 2 · the wowSame score, opposite truth

    Here is the cleanest demonstration that a probe score is ambiguous. Take one ordinary instance-normalising model — Chronos-2 — and ask the probe about two factors. Phase: R² ≈ 0.00. Amplitude: R² ≈ 0.00. Identical verdict — "neither is decodable." A benchmark table would put them in the same bucket and move on.

    The geometry says they are opposites. Sweep the phase and the embedding travels a clean closed ring — present, just circular. Sweep the amplitude across its entire range and the embedding barely stirs: its centroids drift by about 2×10⁻⁵ — while the phase ring spans ten thousand times that. The amplitude knob is all but disconnected from the representation, divided out by the tokenizer before layer 0. (On some models it is even starker: Time-MoE-50M's amplitude centroids are bit-identical to the last decimal at every one of its 13 layers.) One factor is a circle the probe can't read; the other is genuinely gone. Same number, opposite reality.

    And the amplitude verdict isn't about this one model — it's about the tokenizer, decided before the transformer runs. Split the same 37 models by their front-end and amplitude separates into two clean bands at every size: models that keep the raw scale (Toto, Mantis, Sundial, NuTime, …) recover amplitude at R² ≈ 0.99; models that standardise each window (Time-MoE, Chronos, Moirai, Kairos, MOMENT, …) sit on the floor. Parameters don't move the needle; the tokenizer does.

    Case 3 · probe high, incompleteFrequency: when the probe says "solved"

    Not every disagreement is the probe scoring too low. Frequency is the probe's success story — R² 0.99, near-perfectly decodable, and a clean-slice linear readout hits R² 1.0. By the probe's lights, frequency is done. That single number is correct, and it hides the interesting part.

    Look at the shape and the model's frequency "ruler" is a curve coiled ×49 — its arc length is forty-nine times the straight-line distance between its ends — so walking the manifold recovers order that Euclidean distance scrambles (geodesic gain +0.29). And near the Nyquist limit, where a sine is sampled only two-to-four times per cycle, that ruler folds: the high-frequency tail sprays into extra dimensions and loses resolution — the effect we dissected in Part 1. A scalar R² averages right over all of it.

    In contextProbes, attribution, and SAEs

    The three cases map onto a simple grid. A linear probe answers one axis — is it linearly decodable? The geometry answers the other — is it organised the way the world is? Read together they pin down what a single score leaves ambiguous.

    geometry: organised geometry: collapsed
    probe high Frequency. Decodable and structured — but the scalar hides a coil that folds at Nyquist. decodable yet disorganised — a warning the probe alone can't raise.
    probe low Phase. Present but bent into a circle — the probe calls it noise. Amplitude (standardising models). Genuinely gone — probe and geometry agree.

    What about the other tools the question named? Honestly: Aionoscope has run the linear probe — that is the comparison we can make with data — and has not run sparse autoencoders or attribution, so for those we can only reason about what they'd see. Attribution answers a different question — which inputs moved a forecast — not how a factor is arranged inside the representation; it isn't looking at the circle. Sparse autoencoders decompose activations into sparse linear features; a circle is not a sparse linear feature, so an SAE would either miss it or shatter it into many fragmentary atoms — the same blind spot as the probe, at higher cost. The geometry measures organisation and topology directly — order, closure, neighbour faithfulness, intrinsic dimension — which is exactly the axis a linear readout collapses.

    What this shows — and what it doesn't

    • The probe is still the right first instrument: it is fast, assumption-light, and genuinely good at presence. Geometry is a complement, not a replacement — reach for it when a probe says "no" and you need to know why.
    • Geometry is not magic. Case 2's amplitude is the proof: when the information is truly gone, the manifold collapses and agrees with the probe. A clean shape means the factor is there; a collapsed one means it isn't.
    • Scores are best-of-all-layers (Part 1's rationale: the cleanest geometry usually lives mid-network). Probe R² is the benchmark's oracle best-layer value, one factor active (num_enabled = 1) unless noted.
    • It's one synthetic, controlled view — single factor swept on a fine grid, everything else held fixed, at 500 Hz. The verdicts are about these representations under this readout; the dashboard lets you check any model yourself.

    Open research · a community effort

    This is research in progress — come build it with us

    Everything here is early, evolving, and deliberately open. Representation geometry for time-series models is far too interesting — and too big — to keep to ourselves. Collaboration, questions, pushback, and wild ideas are all very welcome.

    • Want a probe-vs-geometry readout for your model? We're happy to add architectures, checkpoints, and families.
    • Think a probe failure is real absence, not geometry? Bring the case — disagreement is how this gets sharper.
    • Have an SAE or attribution comparison to run? We'd love to make the empirical version of this post with you.
    • Spotted a bug or a misleading metric? Tell us — we'll fix it.
